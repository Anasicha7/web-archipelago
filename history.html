Welcome to Our Story

The Dutch governor in Malabar (India) sent arabica coffee (Coffea arabica) seedlings from Yemen to the Dutch governor of Batavia (now Jakarta) in 1696. The first seedlings failed due to flooding in Batavia. The second shipment of seedlings was sent in 1699 with Hendrik Zwaardecroon. The plants grew, and in 1711 the first exports were sent from Java to Europe by the Dutch East India Company—formally Verenigde Oostindische Compagnie and abbreviated VOC—reaching 2,000 pounds shipped in 1717. Coffee arriving in Amsterdam sold for high prices, 1 kg (2 lb) costing nearly one per cent of the average annual income. Indonesia was the first place, outside of Arabia and Ethiopia, where coffee was widely cultivated. The coffee was shipped to Europe from the port of Batavia (now Jakarta). There has been a port at the mouth of Ciliwung River since 397 AD, when King Purnawarman established the city he called Sunda Kelapa. Today, in the City area of Jakarta, one can find echoes of the seagoing legacy that built the city. Sail driven ships still load cargo in the old port. The Bahari museum occupies a former warehouse of the VOC, which was used to store spices and coffee. Menara Syahbandar (or Lookout Tower) was built in 1839 to replace the flag pole that stood at the head of wharves, where the VOC ships docked to load their cargos. In the 18th century, coffee shipped from Batavia sold for 3 Guilders per kilogram in Amsterdam. Since annual incomes in Holland in the 18th century were between 200 and 400 Guilders, this was equivalent to several hundred dollars per kilogram today. By the end of the 18th century, the price had dropped to 0.6 Guilders per kilogram and coffee drinking spread from the elite to the general population. The East Indies were the most important coffee supplier in the world during this period and it was only in the 1840s that their stranglehold on supply was eclipsed by Brazil. The coffee trade was very profitable for the VOC, and for the Dutch East Indies government that replaced it in 1800, but was less so for the Indonesian farmers who were forced to grow it by the colonial government from 1830 to around 1870 under the Cultuurstelsel (Cultivation system).

Brewing Excellence: The Archipelago Coffee Story

Introduction: In the lush landscapes of Indonesia, where fertile soils meet tropical climates, a remarkable tale of coffee excellence unfolds. Archipelago Coffee, a distinguished coffee bean supplier, has emerged as a beacon of quality in the heart of the archipelago.
Founding Journey: Founded [2021], Archipelago Coffee embarked on a journey to redefine the coffee industry. It all began with a vision — a commitment to bring the world the finest coffee beans from the diverse regions of Indonesia.
Our Unique Origins: Nestled among the 17,000 islands of Indonesia, each cup of Archipelago Coffee encapsulates the essence of our rich, volcanic soils and the dedication of local farmers. We source our beans directly from the thriving coffee plantations that dot the landscapes of Sumatra, Java, Bali, and beyond.
Direct Farmer Relationships: At the heart of our story is a commitment to ethical and sustainable sourcing. Archipelago Coffee fosters direct relationships with local farmers, ensuring fair practices and a mutual respect for the craft. This not only empowers local communities but also guarantees a premium product for our customers.
The Art of Roasting: Upon reaching our state-of-the-art roastery, each coffee bean undergoes a meticulous roasting process. Our master roasters, with decades of expertise, bring out the unique flavors and aromas that define Archipelago Coffee. The result is a symphony of taste, a harmonious blend that captures the spirit of Indonesia in every sip.
Sustainability as a Guiding Principle: Beyond delivering exceptional coffee, Archipelago Coffee takes pride in its commitment to sustainability. From environmentally friendly packaging to community development initiatives, we strive to leave a positive impact on both our environment and the lives we touch.
A Global Presence: While deeply rooted in the Indonesian coffee culture, Archipelago Coffee has become a global ambassador for the nation's coffee excellence. Our beans have found homes in cafes, restaurants, and homes around the world, connecting coffee enthusiasts to the unique terroir of Indonesia.
Conclusion: As Archipelago Coffee continues to evolve, our commitment to quality, sustainability, and the vibrant culture of Indonesia remains unwavering. Join us on a journey of taste, where every cup tells a story — a story of passion, craftsmanship, and the extraordinary world of Archipelago Coffee.
